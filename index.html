ForenSonics: Uncovering Evidence of Memorization in Lyrics2Song Models

Jaechul Roh1, Zachary Novack2, Yuefeng Peng1, Niloofar Mireshghallah3, Taylor Berg-Kirkpatrick2, Amir Houmansadr1
1University of Massachusetts Amherst, 2University of California San Diego, 3FAIR, Meta
{jroh, yuefengpeng, amir}@umass.edu

niloofar@cs.washington.edu
Paper arXiv

Abstract

Despite recent claims of robust generalization in lyrics-to-music generative models, we find compelling evidence of regurgitation in YuE, a state-of-the-art lyrics-conditioned music generation model. While YuE asserts minimal memorization, our evaluation reveals that it can regenerate widely known music pieces, including Let It Be, Lose Yourself, and Empire State of Mind, with high fidelity. Strikingly, it also recreates Mandarin and Cantonese copyrighted songs when primed with matching or loosely related lyrics. We introduce a new type of attack: lyrics-conditioned regurgitation, where lyrics themselves can elicit memorized training examples in audio form. Using MiRA, a music retrieval and alignment metric, we show high CLAP similarity scores indicative of audio embedding proximity. We further analyze this memorization through multiple metrics: CoverID, KL divergence, FAD, and a novel DEfNet embedding similarity. Our findings raise critical concerns about the copyright safety of generative music models and challenge the assumption that conditioning on lyrics prevents overfitting. This work motivates stronger auditing tools for generative music models and a reevaluation of current benchmark protocols.
